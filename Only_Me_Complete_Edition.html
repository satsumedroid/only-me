Chapter Two

Borrowed Comfort

I sat up gasping.

The room was dark and silent and completely mine. I pressed both hands flat against the mattress and made myself breathe until the heartbeat settled. A dream. The voice at the end — that low, near-familiar “Oh, really now?” — a dream. The dead do not speak. That is the whole point of what I did. That is the whole point of everything.

(He is gone. I made sure of it. What I am hearing is guilt producing sounds. Guilt is not a ghost. It cannot hurt me.)

I lay back down and waited for morning, and morning came the way it always did in this world: warm, unhurried, with the smell of food rising from below like something designed to make you feel safe.

The days settled into a rhythm. Mother cooked. Father asked about school. Dinner was a conversation, not a performance of one. I was careful. I was precise. Every morning I told myself: you planned this. You are good at this. Stay sharp.

About a week in, I came downstairs to find both my parents on the couch with an old photo album open across their laps. Father was pointing. Mother was already laughing before she saw me.

Mom: “Come look. This is you at six.”

A small boy covered in mud, grinning impossibly wide. I sat down and looked at the photo and kept my face arranged into the right shape.

Dad: “You fell in the ditch trying to catch a frog and came home completely serious, telling us the frog had cheated. You were so offended.”

They both laughed. I laughed one beat after them, which is one beat too late, but not so late that anyone counts.

(That child is not me. That mud is not mine. That grin belongs to a boy who grew up in this house and learned to walk in these halls and called these two people Mom and Dad for his entire life, and I poisoned him over a meal and watched him die and then moved into his room. Me: “I’ve forgotten most of it, honestly. It’s been so long.”

Dad: “That’s okay. You’ve grown up so much. My little frog-chaser.”

He said it with so much uncomplicated love that something behind my sternum moved in a way I had not anticipated and could not fully name. He was aiming all of that at a grave. He just did not know it yet.

(Stop. Stop that. This is the life I chose. These are the terms.)

The friends were harder. Old Friend A and Old Friend B had a shorthand with him built from years of shared stupid moments — inside references and half-finished sentences that landed perfectly between them and reached me like radio signals from a station I couldn’t tune into. Old Friend B: “You’re different lately. Like, a different kind of quiet.”

Me: “People change.”

(Closer than you know.)

Chapter Three

The Shape of Absence

The group project was when I understood the true scale of what I could not take.

Grades: taken. Schedule: taken. Face, name, room, parents, history: taken. But the way he existed between people — the specific voltage of him in a social space, the way his presence made rooms feel louder without him saying anything — that was not a thing that transferred. It was not stored anywhere I could access. It had simply ended with him.

Old Friend A made a half-finished joke. The kind where the punchline is unspoken because the other person is supposed to feel it before it arrives. Both of them looked at me, waiting for whatever came next. Whatever he always said in that slot.

I said something technically correct. The laughter it got was polite. The silence after was brief but total.

(I have his face. I have his grades. I do not have whatever made people lean toward him. That part didn’t come through the mirror. That part stayed with the body.)

Old Friend B held back after the session ended.

Old Friend B: “I’m not trying to be weird about this. I just — are you actually okay? Because you feel like you’re somewhere else even when you’re right here.”

The specific care in his voice — the genuine worry aimed at a person who was no longer in the room to receive it — did something to the inside of my throat. I had not planned for this particular texture of guilt. The kind that comes not from what you took but from who is still looking for it.

Me: “I’m fine. Really.”

He looked at me for a long moment. Then nodded. Then left.

On the walk home I let the thought go all the way to the end for the first time, not stopping it where it became uncomfortable.

(I cannot be him. Not fully. Not in the ways that matter to people who loved him. I can maintain the surface. I can keep the grades and the schedule and the polite smile at dinner. But every time someone who knew him reaches toward who he was, I will not be there. There will be nothing there but the performance of someone trying to be there. And people can feel that gap even when they can’t name it. Old Friend B can feel it. The family feels it. They just keep choosing the explanation they can live with.)

(And there is nothing I can do about any of it. I killed the only person who could have filled that space.)

That was the thought that sat down in me and didn’t move. Not abstract guilt. The specific, surgical understanding that what I destroyed was irreplaceable, and I had known it was irreplaceable, and I had done it anyway.

That night I lay in bed and spoke to the ceiling.

Me: “I’m sorry.”

The silence answered the way silence always does: completely, and without mercy.

But then — under the silence, or inside it, or in the part of me that the silence reached — something shifted. Something that was not me registered the apology. Not with words. With something older than words. A sensation like pressure behind the eyes. Like being recognized.

I sat up in bed.

The room was empty. Of course it was empty. Of course.

Chapter Four

The First Error

I was studying for an exam. Four pages of notes, clean and organized, written in my careful hand. I knew the material. I always knew the material. I closed the notebook and went downstairs for dinner and came back and opened it again for a final review.

Near the bottom of the third page, there was a sentence.

I had not written it.

I knew with complete certainty that I had not written it, the way you know the inside of your own hand. The letters were close to mine — close enough that someone else might not have noticed — but the angle was wrong. The pressure was different. The loops on certain letters did not curve the way mine do. And the content of it, the cold, slightly contemptuous precision of it, was not mine either.

You skipped a step in the derivation. Line 4. Also: I can tell when you’re pretending not to feel things. You’re doing it right now.

I checked line 4. There was an error. A real one, the kind I would have caught on second reading. The sentence had caught it instead.

I sat very still with the notebook in my hands and catalogued every rational explanation available to me. Dissociation. Stress. Unconscious self-correction that I didn’t register writing. The handwriting difference was explainable — angle, fatigue, the pen held differently.

The second half of the sentence was not explainable. I can tell when you’re pretending not to feel things. That was not a study note. That was addressed to me from something that had been watching me.

(He is dead. Dead people don’t observe. Dead people don’t correct homework. Dead people do not know what I am pretending.)

(Dead people do not have opinions about what I feel.)

I corrected line 4. I closed the notebook. I went to bed and told myself what I always told myself when reality presented something I didn’t have a place for: it’s stress, it’s tiredness, there’s an explanation, I just don’t have it yet.

In the morning the sentence was still there. Exactly as I had left it. The handwriting had not changed back overnight the way I had half-hoped it might.

I sat at the desk and wrote my own name in my own hand and compared the two. They were not the same hand.

(Okay.)

(Okay. I’ll figure this out. I always figure things out.)

I said it until I believed it, which took longer than usual. Then I got dressed and went to school and performed a normal day with the thoroughness of someone who understood that performance was now load-bearing.

On the way home, for the first time, I felt watched from inside my own body.

Chapter Five

Echoes

The handwriting came back. Not every day. Enough.

In the margins of my notes: corrections, additions, observations. Always accurate. Always in that hand that was almost mine but colder, more angular, as though written by someone who had learned my penmanship from watching rather than from being me. The content shifted over time. At first it was only academic. Then it became something else.

You flinched when father touched your shoulder. He noticed. He didn’t say anything because he’s kind. That’s the difference between us. He was always the one people wanted to protect.

You answered Old Friend A wrong today. The real answer was funnier. He would have known that. You don’t know that.

I’m not haunting you. I want to be clear about that. I don’t know what this is either. But I am here and you know I’m here and I think that’s going to be a problem for you.

I started the journal on a Thursday. A separate notebook, bought with my own money, kept in the back of the desk drawer. Date, time, observation. I was going to document this methodically. I was going to find the mechanism and address it. I was not going to let something I didn’t understand yet become something that undid me.

The teacher incident happened the following Monday.

I was mid-answer in class when he held up his hand.

Teacher: “You already told me this. Yesterday. After class. You came to my desk and explained this exact sequence for almost ten minutes.”

I had not stayed after class. I had gone directly home, eaten dinner, studied, gone to bed. My memory of the afternoon was complete and unambiguous.

Me: “I think there might be a misunderstanding, sir.”

Teacher: “I remember it very clearly.”

The classroom was quiet. Twenty faces waiting. I kept my expression neutral because the alternative was letting the thing behind my face show, and I was not yet ready to name what the thing behind my face was.

(Something was at that desk yesterday. In my body, with my voice, explaining things I would have explained exactly that way. And I was not there for it. I was somewhere else. Which means for some portion of yesterday afternoon, I was not the one driving.)

Me: “I apologize for the confusion, sir.”

Then Old Friend A was mid-sentence about something and I answered the question before he finished it. Clear and correct. As though I had heard it before. Because some part of me had — some part of me that listened to things before I did, that watched what was coming before I could see it.

Old Friend A: “How did you know what I was going to say?”

Me: “Lucky guess.”

That night in the journal, I wrote for a long time. Then I read the last entry back and the handwriting on the final two lines was not mine.

You should be more scared than you are. I’m not saying that to be cruel. I’m saying it because the fact that you’re not scared enough is probably going to cost you.

I stared at those two lines for a very long time. Then I wrote in my own hand directly beneath them:

What do you want.

I waited.

No answer came that night. But in the morning, the journal had one new line.

That’s a better question than “what are you.” I’ll give you that.

He was learning how to talk to me. That was the part that kept me awake that night.

Chapter Six

The Reflection Delay

Saturday morning. Half awake. The bathroom. Brush teeth, look up.

My hand was down. Mouth closed. Arm at my side.

In the mirror, the reflection was still brushing.

Three full seconds of it — slow, deliberate circles, completely unaware that I had stopped. Then it caught up. Hand down. Perfectly synchronized. Looking back at me with my own eyes, exactly as it always had.

I stood completely still and looked at my own face in the glass and tried to determine which of us was doing the looking.

(Visual lag. Sleep deprivation. The brain processing input in the wrong order. There is a documented phenomenon. This is that phenomenon.)

I rinsed and left and did not look back.

Three days later I was getting dressed. I reached for my collar. And in the mirror, the reflection turned its head.

Before I did.

Not a lag. An anticipation. It knew I was going to turn and it turned first, as though it were making a decision rather than reflecting one. I froze. It froze too. We stood looking at each other and the image in the glass was perfectly still and perfectly normal and I could not stop seeing the moment before it, the fraction of a second where the reflection acted and I followed.

(He is in there. He is in the glass because he is in me and the reflection is reflecting both of us and sometimes he gets there first. He doesn’t speak yet. He just… moves. Sometimes before I do.)

I bought a cloth and covered the bedroom mirror that evening. Mother asked why.

Me: “The light angle gives me headaches in the morning.”

She accepted this. I was grateful for how much people will accept when the alternative requires them to worry.

I started avoiding the bathroom mirror too. I got very good at brushing teeth by feel. I developed the habit of looking at my hands while I washed them rather than up at the glass.

Every night before bed I wrote in the journal: reflection: no change.

What I meant was: it has not looked back at me with its own expression yet. It has not smiled when I wasn’t smiling. I am counting the nights it has not done this because when I stop being able to write that it will be the clearest evidence yet of how bad things have become.

But I did not write all of that. I just wrote: reflection: no change.

(He is getting stronger. I felt it when I got to the desk chair this morning — the slight resistance, like moving through water. As though my body considered two directions before choosing mine. He couldn’t hold it. But he considered it.)

I started doing things faster. Making decisions before I could feel him considering them. Preempting. Racing myself to my own body.

Chapter Seven

Memory Bleed

The dreams started and they were not nightmares. That was the worst part. They were warm.

His grandmother’s birthday. A park route he had memorized before he could read. An argument with his father over curfew where his father eventually laughed and gave in. I woke from these not with the gasping disorient of nightmares but with the slow, heavy surfacing of someone who had been somewhere real. The memories settled in my body the way lived memories do, with texture and weight and the particular kind of certainty that belongs only to things that actually happened.

By the end of the second week, I knew things I had no right to know.

I ducked slightly passing a shelf in the school library without thinking about it. The broken bracket that would have caught my sleeve was exactly where I knew it would be, because he had caught his sleeve on it a dozen times, and now so had I, in a memory I hadn’t lived. I knew which corner of the cafeteria had the bad lighting and which teacher preferred questions before class rather than after. I knew the specific pitch of Old Friend A’s laugh when something genuinely surprised him versus when he was being polite.

Mother served something at breakfast and I ate it without comment.

Mom: “You used to make a face every time I made this. You called it ‘punishment food.’”

Me: “I don’t remember that.”

And I genuinely did not. Whatever visceral aversion he had built for it was not in the memories that arrived. I had received the route and the library shelf and the friends’ laughs, but not the tastes and the textures, not the embodied things. His memories came without his body’s reactions. They were his mind without his nerves. His map without his feet.

One afternoon at the bench, Old Friend A started complaining and I said the exact response before I consciously planned it. The right timing, the right delivery, the specific joke that landed. Old Friend A went quiet for a second and then laughed — a real laugh, the surprised kind.

Old Friend A: “You haven’t said that in months. I thought you retired it.”

(I didn’t say it. He said it. Through me. I was the medium and he was the message and it lasted about two seconds and neither of them noticed the seam where he ended and I began because there was no seam. Not anymore. Not in those two seconds.)

That night I wrote in the journal and the entry was mine until the last paragraph, where the handwriting shifted.

You used my joke. I’m not angry. I just want you to understand what that means. The things you know that you shouldn’t — routes, habits, punch lines — those are mine. You didn’t earn them. You absorbed them. And you’re going to keep absorbing them. This is what ‘sharing a body’ actually means. Not just the space. Everything.

I read that three times. Then I wrote beneath it:

Does that mean I’m losing myself? Or gaining you?

The answer came in the morning.

Yes.

One word. No elaboration. As though the question was not complicated enough to require one.

Chapter Eight

Compression

I sat at the desk for forty minutes before I picked up the pen. This was not reluctance. It was the specific dread of writing something that, once written, cannot be unwritten. Once I put it on the page I owned it. Once I owned it I had to act on it.

I picked up the pen.

He is not haunting me. Haunting is something that happens from outside. What is happening is inside. He didn’t go away when his body died. He came with me through the mirror. He is in here. We are in the same body, in the same space, and the space is his original space, which means I am the one who doesn’t fit. I removed him from his life. I did not remove him from himself. I just removed the space between us. We are the same location now. One body. Two people. And one of us is getting stronger.

I put the pen down. Read what I’d written. The handwriting was entirely mine. No intrusions this time. He had let me write it myself because he wanted me to own it. That was the kind of cruelty I was beginning to recognize as his style — quiet, patient, surgical.

The impulses had been building for weeks. I would reach for something and feel a second opinion about which hand to use. I would form a sentence and feel an alternative version rising beneath it. They were not voices. There was no voice. It was more like being a river with a current running through it that was not quite the same current. Sometimes the currents aligned. Sometimes one pushed against the other and you could feel the friction without being able to see it.

(I am not losing my mind. I know exactly what is happening. What is happening is that there are two of us in here and we are not compatible and neither of us can leave.)

I wrote one more line in the journal.

I didn’t remove him. I removed the space between us. And I don’t know what happens next to either of us.

The handwriting on the word either shifted, almost imperceptibly, before shifting back.

He was reading along. He always read along.

(I know you’re reading this.)

I didn’t write that in the journal. I thought it very clearly and directly, aimed inward, the way you aim a thought at a person in the room with you. A beat of silence. Then the faintest sensation of being acknowledged.

Not words. But not nothing either.

The first time we communicated directly. Neither of us chose it. Neither of us was ready.

Chapter Nine

Internal Battle: Part 1

Once he knew that I knew, the pretense ended.

The handwriting in the margins stopped. The subtle pushes against my decisions stopped. What replaced them was direct, immediate, and considerably harder to manage.

He tried to take the body.

The first time was in the morning. I was reaching for my school bag and the arm moved wrong — not toward the bag but toward the desk, deliberate and purposeful, going somewhere I hadn’t decided to go. I pulled back. There was a moment of genuine resistance, two intentions occupying the same limb, the physical sensation of it like trying to open a door that is also being pushed shut from the other side. Then my direction won. The arm went to the bag.

I stood in the middle of the room breathing hard.

(He’s trying to take control. Actually trying. Not just influencing. Not just writing in margins. He wants the body.)

He didn’t get it. Not for lack of trying but because the body wasn’t his anymore. He could get a limb. He could get a few seconds. But he couldn’t hold it.

Not yet.

The attempts kept coming. On the way to school: the legs wanted to turn left when I turned right, and I felt the disagreement in my knees. During class: my hand began writing something I hadn’t thought of, and I pressed the pen hard against the paper to reassert. During dinner: my mouth almost said something that would have confused my parents badly, and I caught it just in time and took a long drink of water.

The strain of it was exhausting in a way I had no framework for. This was not the exhaustion of effort or of stress. It was the exhaustion of having to consciously inhabit yourself. Of never, at any point, being able to simply be without checking that the self doing the being was you.

Dinner was harder than anything else. The family asked questions — ordinary ones, the conversational texture of an evening meal — and I had to answer them with part of my attention while the rest of my attention held the body’s controls, felt for any drift, corrected for any push. I was monitoring two things simultaneously: the performance of a son at the table, and the quiet war beneath it. Father made a joke. I laughed exactly one beat late. Mother watched my face for half a second longer than usual.

(She noticed. She’ll attribute it to tiredness. She usually does. I need to be better tomorrow. I need to be more present. But being more present requires attention I am currently spending on not letting someone else use my hands at the dinner table.)

The mental toll compounded. By the end of the week I was making errors in class. Small ones, uncharacteristic. My concentration fractured at unpredictable moments. I arrived at the ends of conversations with no memory of the middles, which was visible in ways I couldn’t fully manage.

(He’s wearing me down. He can’t take the body directly but he’s weakening it. Every failed attempt costs both of us something and he knows I can’t afford to keep paying.)

I started sleeping less. Specifically to stay sharp. Specifically to prevent him from using the gap of unconsciousness to do things I wouldn’t know about. This was not a sustainable strategy, which I knew, but sustainability had become a secondary concern.

The necklace was still on the desk, where it had been since the night I thought I’d destroyed it and found I hadn’t. I had put a book on top of it. I had not moved the book. But I was aware of it the way you are aware of a bruise — not constantly, but whenever something brushed near it.

By Thursday of that week something happened that I could not categorize as manageable.

It started in class. A question directed at me, ordinary, the kind I had always answered without effort. I opened my mouth and nothing came. Not hesitation — absence. The words that should have been there were not there. The class was quiet. The teacher waited. I sat in the chair and felt the body as something I did not have secure access to, as something currently occupied by competing claims that were both refusing to speak.

The teacher moved on. I sat there for the rest of the period with my hands flat on the desk and focused on the single, narrowing task of remaining present in myself.

On the way home I stopped on the street with the broken tile and could not continue walking for approximately two minutes. Not because of anything outside. Because continuing required deciding which direction was mine and both directions felt equally wrong, equally unfamiliar, equally someone else’s.

I stood on the street and breathed and told myself: I am here. I chose this. I am not going anywhere. I am the one in control.

The words had been reliable once. They were less reliable now.

I got home. I went upstairs. I sat on the floor of the bedroom with my back against the bed and did not move for a long time. Not writing. Not thinking. Not managing anything. Just sitting, with the specific quality of someone who has stopped being able to perform even the minimum and is waiting to find out whether that is temporary.

Mother knocked on the door after a while.

Mom: “Dinner’s ready.”

Me: “I’m not hungry tonight. Sorry.”

A pause.

Mom: “Are you sure? You haven’t eaten since—”

Me: “I’m sure. I just need to rest.”

Another pause. Longer. Then the sound of her footsteps going back down the hall, quieter than her footsteps usually were, the specific quiet of someone moving carefully around something they don’t want to disturb.

(She is worried. She has been worried for weeks. She keeps the worry small and available, ready to be offered when I give her an opening, which I don’t. I have not given her a real opening since the photo album. I have been managing distance instead of distance managing itself, and I am now sitting on a floor because I could not continue walking down a street, and the only reason I am not more alarmed about that is that I don’t have the capacity for alarm right now either.)

I sat on the floor until the house went quiet. Then I got up. Got into bed. Lay in the dark.

The thought arrived without announcement: I might lose this. Not because someone finds out. Because I run out.

I had not thought that before. I had thought about exposure, about the twin, about the cracks, about all the external threats. I had not thought about the possibility that the thing most likely to end this was simply the accumulation of cost on a person who had finite capacity for it. I had been thinking about this as a problem to be solved. It was also something happening to a body and a mind that had limits, and those limits were visible from where I was sitting, and the visibility of them was new.

One evening after dinner I sat at the desk and thought at him, directly, the way I had learned to:

(You can’t hold it. It cuts off every time. You know that.)

The answer came as a sensation rather than words. Something between amusement and patience. The feeling of someone who has decided to wait you out because they know something you don’t, and they are comfortable in the knowing, and they have decided that letting you figure it out yourself will be instructive.

I did not sleep well that night. He did not need sleep. That was the first asymmetry I had not accounted for.

Chapter Ten

Internal Battle: Part 2

Two weeks into the open war, he found the method.

It was not strength. It was subtlety. He could not overpower me when I was alert and rested and fully engaged with the act of being here. But when I was stressed — genuinely stressed, the kind that made everything loosen its grip slightly — the resistance dropped. The door I held shut by vigilance cracked open. And he was through it before I could close it again.

The bad day that gave him his first real foothold was ordinary in its badness. An exam result lower than expected. A comment from father at dinner that was kind but landed somewhere tender. And, between those two: an encounter with Old Friend B that I am still turning over because of what it showed me about how far things had slipped without my noticing.

He had found me in the hallway after lunch, which meant he had looked for me, which meant he had made a decision to do this.

Old Friend B: “Hey. Can we talk for a second?”

Me: “Sure.”

Old Friend B: “I sent you three messages this week. You left them on read.”

I had not left them on read deliberately. I had read them and then something in the management of the day had consumed whatever I would have used to respond, and by the time I remembered, responding felt like trying to reopen a window that had already closed.

Me: “I’m sorry. I’ve been—”

Old Friend B: “Busy, I know. You’ve been busy for months. I just — I don’t know what to do with you lately. I don’t know if you want me around or if I’m just someone you’re tolerating until whatever you’re dealing with goes away.”

He said it without anger. That was the thing that made it hard to deflect. Anger would have been easier. This was just someone telling me something true about how they felt, with the specific vulnerability of someone who had decided the truth was worth the risk of saying it.

(He is asking me if I still want him here. The answer is yes. The answer is one of the few clear yeses I have. But I cannot explain why the answer is yes in a way that doesn’t also require explaining everything else, and everything else is not something I can explain.)

Me: “I want you around. I’m sorry I haven’t shown it.”

Old Friend B: “Okay.” He looked at me for a moment. “Are you going to tell me what’s actually going on?”

Me: “Not yet. Maybe eventually.”

He nodded slowly. The nod of someone accepting something they’re not satisfied with because the alternative is pushing too hard and he didn’t want to push too hard.

Old Friend B: “Okay. But I’m still here. Even when you’re… whatever this is.”

He left. I stood in the hallway and understood, with a clarity that the rest of the day’s noise had not allowed, that what he had just said was one of the most generous things anyone had said to me in this world, and I had given him almost nothing in return, and he was still going to be there, and the fact that he was going to be there was something I did not deserve and would probably lose anyway through the accumulation of all the things I could not tell him.

By the time I got to my room that evening I was frayed in a way that showed in the body’s grip on itself.

I sat down at the desk to write in the journal. The next thing I was aware of was standing at the window in the dark with my hand against the glass, looking at the street below, with no memory of getting up.

He had had the body for approximately forty minutes.

I checked the journal. There was an entry in his handwriting:

HimForty-three minutes. I didn’t do anything with it. I just wanted you to know I could. I walked to the window. I looked at the street. I thought about how it used to be mine. How I used to look at this same street from this same window and it was just — mine. No maintenance required. No war. Just mine. I wanted to feel what that felt like again. Even for forty-three minutes. I think you understand that, actually. Better than you’d like to.

I read that three times. Then I wrote beneath it:

If you do anything that makes the family suspicious I will find a way to make this worse for you.

His response came in the morning, while I was getting dressed and trying not to look at the covered mirror:

HimYou can’t make things worse for me. I’m already dead and sharing a body with my killer. You, on the other hand, have a family downstairs eating breakfast. You have things to lose. I don’t. Keep that asymmetry in mind when you’re deciding how to talk to me.

He was right. That was the infuriating part. He had nothing left to protect. He was already past every threshold of loss. I was the one maintaining a performance, a cover, a life that required his cooperation or at least his non-interference to continue. My vulnerability was my investment in the outcome.

I started managing my emotional state the way you manage an injury in a location you cannot stop using. Carefully. Constantly. Limiting the situations that could compromise it. I slept at consistent times. I ate well. I avoided situations with unpredictable emotional weight. I became, in the pursuit of self-preservation, a very well-regulated person.

The family noticed this too, but differently from before. Before, the inconsistencies had been of personality — the wrong reaction, the wrong preference. Now they were of availability. I was present at the table but not fully present. I answered correctly but slightly late. I smiled at the right things but the smile didn’t go all the way in.

Mother, one evening: “Are you sleeping well? You seem tired in a way that sleep doesn’t fix.”

Me: “I’m fine. School is just busy.”

She accepted this. She was very good at accepting things. But I noticed she refilled my tea without being asked, and she watched me over the rim of her cup in the way parents watch when they have decided not to push but have not decided not to worry.

(I am losing the dinner table. Not because anyone found out. Because managing him takes the part of me that used to be relaxed at dinner, and relaxed is what the table needs, and now I bring something different and they can feel the difference even when they can’t name it.)

He adapted too. Without the stress trigger being as available, he found smaller gaps. Moments of genuine distraction. Moments when my attention was fully occupied elsewhere and the grip on the body loosened by default. He used them not to act dramatically but to establish presence. A sentence inserted into a classroom discussion that I hadn’t planned. A detour on the walk home that I became aware of only when the street was wrong. Small enough that each instance was explainable. Together, they were building something.

Father asked one morning if I remembered a conversation from the previous evening. I had no memory of it. I said I must have been half-asleep, which was the kind of explanation that works once. Mother mentioned I had said something strange to her about the garden — something fond and specific, about a plant she’d had since before he was born — and she had been pleased by it and a little surprised. I had not been in the kitchen when this apparently happened.

He was talking to them. When he had the body, he wasn’t wasting it on gestures. He was talking to my family, in my voice, building a version of me in their memories that I couldn’t fully see or track or correct for. A version that knew about the garden. That had the right warmth at the right moments. That was, perhaps, more like the person they remembered than I had managed to be.

(He’s not trying to expose me. He’s trying to reclaim them. He’s trying to be their son again, in whatever increments are available to him. And the increments are working. The look on mother’s face when she told me about the garden comment. That look was not meant for me.)

I wrote in the journal that night:

You’re trying to surface. To exist to them in the spaces I leave. To be present to them in ways I can’t manage anymore.

His answer came the next morning, and it was one line, and it was the line I least wanted to read:

HimI’m trying to go home. You of all people should understand that.

I sat with that for a long time. The journal open on the desk. The house quiet around me. His answer sitting on the page in his handwriting, which looked more like mine every week, which was its own particular horror.

I understood exactly. That was the problem.

Chapter Eleven

The Other

Old Friend B found me at the bench on a Thursday.

Not at lunch — after school, which was unusual. He sat down without asking if it was okay, which meant he had been thinking about this for long enough that he’d moved past the stage of asking. He had a specific look, the one he got when he had rehearsed something and was now committed to saying it regardless of how it landed.

Old Friend B: “I want to ask you something and I want you to actually answer it. Not the answer you give when you’re trying to make someone stop worrying.”

Me: “Okay.”

Old Friend B: “What happened to you?”

He said it simply. No preamble. Just the question, direct, aimed at me the way he’d aimed questions at me since Chapter Three when he first said I felt like I was somewhere else even when I was right here. He had been building toward this for months. He had been patient about it. The patience was ending.

Old Friend B: “Not what’s wrong at school. Not stress or growing up or whatever. Something happened. I can see it. I’ve been seeing it since the start of the semester and I kept thinking you’d come back but you didn’t come back, you got further away, and now it’s… I don’t know. I don’t know who I’m talking to half the time. And I miss him. I miss whoever you were. Is that stupid?”

He looked at me with the particular steadiness of someone who has decided that the truth is worth the risk of being dismissed for saying it. And then he did something he almost never did: he looked away. Down at the bench surface. Because the thing he had said was exposing and he had said it anyway and now he was waiting to see whether it had been worth it.

(He misses him. He is sitting on this bench missing a person who is dead, telling the wrong person about it, and he doesn’t know that’s what he’s doing. He is grieving, in the specific form of grief that doesn’t know it’s grief because the person is still standing there saying the right words at the right intervals. And the care in it — that he showed up and said this out loud even knowing I might deflect — that is exactly the kind of care that was aimed at the person I killed. And I am receiving it wrong, in the wrong body, with the wrong history, and there is no way to tell him any of that.)

(I want to tell him. That is the part I cannot afford to look at directly. I want to sit on this bench and say: you’re right. Something happened. I am not who you think I am. He is gone and I am so sorry and I don’t know how to fix it and I am so tired of this.)

Me: “It’s not stupid.”

Old Friend B looked back up.

Me: “I’m sorry. For all of it. For being away. For the messages. For —” I stopped. There was no ending to that sentence that was honest. “I can’t explain what happened. I know that’s not what you want to hear. But I want you to know it’s not about you. You didn’t do anything wrong. You’ve done everything right.”

He was quiet for a moment.

Old Friend B: “Okay.” Then: “I’m still here. That option is still open. Whenever you want to use it.”

He got up and left. I stayed on the bench until the school grounds emptied around me. The afternoon light went long and thin across the concrete and I sat in it and let myself feel, for the first time without managing it, the full weight of what the bench represented.

(I chose this life because I wanted people in it. People who would notice. People who would stay even when I was difficult. And I have that person, right here, and I have never been further from being able to use it. The thing I killed someone for is the thing I cannot have. That is not a new understanding. But sitting here, after what he just said, it is the first time the understanding has felt like weight in my chest rather than a conclusion in my head. Those are different things. I did not know they were different until right now.)

I walked home. Inside me, he was quiet — the twin, listening from wherever he lived in the body’s interior, silent with the quality of someone who recognized something and was not sure what to do with the recognition.

HimHe was my friend first.

(I know.)

HimHe would have done the same for me. He did do the same for me. Before.

(I know that too.)

HimHe deserves the truth.

(Everyone I care about deserves the truth. That has nothing to do with whether I can give it to them.)

Neither of us said anything after that. We walked the rest of the way home in the silence of two people who have been reminded of something they share that is not the war.

• • •

We were both in the body when the other version appeared. That was becoming more normal — both present, neither fully dominant, a kind of tense shared occupancy that cost me energy and, I suspected, frustrated him enormously. The walk home. The usual route. The street with the broken tile.

He was standing across the road.

My face. My height. My build. Completely still on the opposite pavement, looking directly at us as though he had been waiting there for some time and had already decided not to run.

I stopped. Inside me, he stopped too — the simultaneous arrest, both of us recognizing the same impossible thing at the same moment, neither of us having a word for it.

The figure across the street was afraid. Not threatened. Not aggressive. Afraid with the settled quality of someone who has been afraid for so long it has become structural — built into the posture, into the way he held his shoulders already braced, into the eyes that were very still in a face that was trying not to show anything.

And then he walked away. Quickly. Head down.

We stood at the corner for a moment. Then I started walking. He didn’t push for control. He was too occupied with what we had both just seen.

That night the journal entry was mine until it wasn’t:

There is another one. My face. He was afraid. He is in the same position you were — someone crossed over into his world and took his life. Another artifact. Another crossing. The necklace said nothing about where displaced people go. I assumed they just — stopped. Were absorbed somewhere. Ceased. I did not think about what it would mean for them to land somewhere real and keep existing in it.

His handwriting cut into the paragraph:

HimI didn’t know either. I thought the same thing you thought — that displacement was clean. That the original just went somewhere unspecific and that was the end of it. The necklace let me believe that because I wanted to believe it. I think that is the thing about the artifact that neither of us understood until now: it doesn’t lie. It just doesn’t volunteer the parts you would prefer not to know.

We sat with that together — which was a strange thing to experience, sitting with something together. Two people in one body, sharing the same silence, both looking at the same wall, both turning over the same understanding. Not at peace. Not comfortable. But for one specific moment, genuinely in the same situation with the same information and the same unresolved feeling about what the information meant.

(For what it’s worth. I didn’t know displacing you would send you somewhere. I thought you’d just stop. I was wrong. I’m not sure that changes anything. But I was wrong about that specific part.)

A beat. Then, from him, in the space between thoughts rather than in the journal:

HimI know. That doesn’t change what you did. But I know.

The most honest exchange we had ever had. Neither of us had chosen it. It had arrived because something outside us was bigger than the war between us, and for one moment the war had simply — paused.

Chapter Twelve

Lots of Me

There was another one on Wednesday. Two more on Friday. By the following week I had counted seven.

Chapter Twenty

Final Choice

I woke up and the ceiling was familiar.

I got out of bed. Made it — flat and neat, corners tucked the way I had learned to like them. Went downstairs. The kitchen smelled like eggs and something sweet. Mother was humming something without a title. Father was reading. I sat down and ate breakfast slowly, tasting it properly, not because I was performing attention but because I had decided to actually be present for this. The weight of the fork. The quality of the morning light. The exact pitch of the hum.

Me: “Good morning.”

Mother looked up with her good-morning smile. Father nodded from behind his pages. Ordinary. Unaware. The breakfast table doing what breakfast tables do, completely indifferent to the fact that the person sitting at it had killed more people than he would ever count.

After breakfast I went to school. Walked the route. The broken tile on the corner. The house with the dog. No cracks today — not because they were gone but because I had spent the predawn hours closing the ones that had opened overnight. The work of it was familiar now, like any practiced task. I barely thought about what I was doing anymore. That was the part I tried not to think about too carefully.

I sat through lectures. I answered correctly. I walked past Old Friend A and Old Friend B in the hall and they waved and I waved back and there was a version of ease in it by now — not the ease of genuine belonging but the ease of long practice. The ease of knowing the choreography so well you can perform it without any awareness of performing.

After school. The park. The bench. The fountain.

This was where the days of normalcy ended, because I needed to be somewhere that was his before it was mine, to do what I had decided to do. I had been avoiding the third option for months. Not because I didn’t understand it. Because I understood it completely.

The necklace was in my pocket. It had been there all day, warm, patient, waiting.

I sat on the bench and looked at the fountain and let myself think about what I had thought I would never allow myself to think. Specifically. All the way to the end.

(The doubles are infinite. The realities are infinite. I am not. The kills slow the destruction but they do not stop it, and the number required to slow it grows faster than I can kill. This is not a problem I can solve by staying in it. The only person whose death closes a crack completely and permanently — not just seals it but removes it — is me. One version of me, stepping into the crack rather than through it. Into the fracture. Becoming the seal.)

(The necklace showed this. One person, one scene, different from all the others. They stepped in. And in the aftermath the other cracks — not all of them, not instantly, but visibly, measurably — dimmed.)

(I have been making the problem smaller by killing. I can make the problem end by going.)

The fountain ran. A bird. The late-afternoon light on the water doing what late-afternoon light does, indifferent and beautiful.

I thought about the breakfast table. I thought about my father’s uncomplicated pride and my mother’s hum without a title and the way Old Friend B’s worry had felt like something I didn’t deserve, aimed at a person who wasn’t there to receive it, and how I had tried to become that person anyway and how far I had managed to close the distance before the distance proved unclosable.

I thought about him. Not the twin as opponent, not the twin as the body’s competitor, but the boy in the mud in the photo album, age six, grinning with the specific whole-body confidence of a person who has not yet learned that the world can be unkind. The person my mother hummed for. The person my father’s pride had been aimed at for eighteen years.

I had not been able to be him. But I had been able to be here, in his place, long enough to understand what I had taken. That was the thing I had refused to look at and that I was now looking at directly, without blinking, because there was no reason left not to.

(I wanted a better life. I got one, and it was borrowed, and everything I touched in it was already someone else’s. And I did not deserve it, and I knew I did not deserve it, and I chose it anyway, and the choice broke something whose breakage I could not fix by staying.)

I took the necklace out of my pocket.

A crack appeared in the air above the fountain. Wide. Open longer than any I had seen. On its far side: my face, younger-looking, afraid. Not one of the mapped doubles. A new one. Someone who had just arrived and had not yet learned to be afraid quietly.

I stood. Walked toward the crack. Not toward him — the figure stepped back, uncertain — but toward the fracture itself. Toward the space between.

The necklace was warm in my closed hand. Warmer than it had ever been. As though it had always known this was where we were going and was ready for it.

———

What the sacrifice looked like from the outside, if anyone had been watching:

A person standing at the edge of a fountain in the late afternoon, holding something small in his closed hand. Then not standing there anymore. No transition visible. No light, no sound, no theatre. Just the bench, and the fountain, and the afternoon, and an absence shaped exactly like a person.

The crack above the fountain sealed immediately. Completely. Not the gradual narrowing of a mended fracture but an instant total closure, as though the fracture had never existed.

In the weeks that followed, the other cracks across the city dimmed. Slowed. Some closed entirely. The versions of people stopped appearing in hallways and parks and across streets. The air of the world went back to the ordinary quality of air that does not have something wrong with it.

Nobody knew why. Nobody had been able to see the problem in the first place. The solution was equally invisible. The world healed the way the world tends to: without acknowledgment, without ceremony, as though the injury had never been significant enough to name.

———

And on the other side of the choice:

No ceiling. No room. No morning light deciding which way to fall.

No family. No table. No hum without a title.

No friends whose grief could not find its target.

No other voice. No second current. No shared space, no equal light.

No versions of a face scattered across the surface of a cracking world.

No cracks.

No world to crack.

Just space. Unbounded. Unwitnessed. Without dimension or direction or the particular quality of being observed.

Just this.

Just here.

Just —

No one else. Not anymore. Only me.

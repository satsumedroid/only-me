Chapter One

New Life

[ Chapter One — as written by the author ]

I woke up, feeling refreshed. This is the first time I’ve woken up and looked forward to the rest of the day. I looked up and saw a kind of unfamiliar ceiling. The ray of sunshine passes through the window, hitting my face preventing me from sleeping more. I stayed in bed for a few minutes, waiting for a certain feeling to come.

It did not come.

There’s no dreadful or weird feeling. It is just a normal everyday morning. I got up the bed and stretched for a bit before going downstairs to eat breakfast. I saw mother preparing breakfast. I’m not the one making my own. It seems that this is reality. Not a dream. I’m really in a better place.

While I was contemplating the reality of the situation, I hear movement, plates clinking and soft humming. The smell of food reached my nose before I even arrived at the table. Eggs, toast maybe, and something sweet.

“Good morning” mother said with a smile. The kind of smile that is sweet and kind. Not like my other mother. “You’re up quite early.”

“I’m kind of excited for school today so I woke up early. It is a new semester.” I said. I’m truly excited for today since it is a new semester like I said and also the first day of my permanent life in this world.

“It is a big day today so eat your fill. I’m going to take you to school today since it is my vacation for a week. We can bond for a while, like we used to.”

Right. There’s also father. A person that is absent in my life before. Now I also have one. It’s nice having a whole loving family. Both mother and father present. I wonder if this is how he felt when they are together. The other me. No, no, no. It’s no use thinking about it. He’s already gone.

As I was having my internal monologue, I am kind of spacing out. My father thought that I don’t like the idea of him taking me to school.

“It’s okay if you don’t want me to do it.” My father said in a kind of sad tone.

“Ah. No, I’d love to. I was just preoccupied with some useless thoughts. I’m not unwilling at all.” I said with a smile.

There’s no way I would say no to that. That would be stupid. I should remove such useless thoughts. I’ll forget the memories of mine from the other world and make new ones. Happy ones with my family.

“Okay, that’s great.” he said with a smile.

After having that conversation, we started eating. I ate slowly, since I want to savor the time eating with my family. Although this is not a new experience since I already experienced it before. With the switching shenanigans. But this one is special because, like I said before, a start of the new life.

“After you are done eating, just call me. I will be in the garage preparing the car. Okay?” father said. And I did just that. After I finished eating, I prepared the things I need to bring to school. Then I went to the front yard where father is waiting. He looked kind of nervous. Went inside the car and father drove to school. While on the way, father asked various questions and talked a lot.

“Uhm, how was your school lately?” father asked.

“It’s great. I’m having a great time at school. Everyone is nice to me for the past month.” I replied.

“Really?!? That’s great. You really have changed. I was kinda surprised when you started studying seriously and thought that it would go away quickly. But you stick with it. I am so proud of you. You were kind of an unruly kid before so you don’t get along well with others. Since you’ve changed, you’re able to get along with others now. What made you decide to turn into a new leaf anyway?”

“Just realized that I may have taken things for granted. You work hard to provide for our family, and here I am, wasting all of it by playing around. Don’t worry father, I am a new person now.”

“I’m not gonna dwell too much on the reasons. Everything is good so let’s end it there. And we’re about to arrive at your school.”

I just smiled and did not continue the conversation until we reached the school. After father left, I stayed at the school gate for a few seconds, staring and imagining great things that waited ahead of me. Of course, I got a few ridiculing stares since I’m giggling like an idiot. I woke up from my daydreaming and went inside the school. Then, my “old friends” saw me and decided to talk with me. I don’t know any of them though. I never met them in the past 3 months that we’ve been switching. Perhaps he arranged it that way because I would get found out that I’m not him if I talk to them.

Old Friend A: “Hey, wanna hang out after school? Or just after lunch since it is just first day. Nothing to do yet.”

MC: “… Today?”

Old Friend B: “Yeah, like what you said before, ‘playing around is every other day only starting from now on’. You said few months ago. You have important things to do you said.”

(He really did arrange it that way.)

MC: “I think I’ll pass for today. I got lots to do today and there is a test so…”

Old Friend A: “Seriously? Now that you’ve mentioned it, what’s up with a test around here on the first day? That’s dumb.”

Old Friend B: “Your loss man. Although tell us if you change your mind. We’ll be waiting.”

MC: “Okay. Thanks.”

Their words stayed longer than it should have. For a brief moment, an image popped up in my mind. Someone careless, loud, and always laughing. Someone who complained about his parents being too restrictive and kept goofing around with his friends. Sure he’s an arrogant asshole. But there’s someone who cares for him. Those feelings tightened my chest. Then I exhaled. The guilt lingered, even though it is thin and fragile. So I just brushed it off. There’s no point mourning a life that was never mine.

After school I got the highest score in class. It feels nice. I arrived home, studied for a bit before eating dinner with my parents. I am kind of silent while eating so my parents got a bit worried.

Mom: “Hey, are you okay?”

Me: “I’m fine. I am just thinking of something. You don’t have anything to worry about.”

I ate and went to my room and straight to bed.

Me: “He really does have a good life. Loving family, good friends. I feel bad for him now.”

Chapter Two

Borrowed Comfort

I sat up gasping.

The room was dark and silent and completely mine. I pressed both hands flat against the mattress and made myself breathe until the heartbeat settled. A dream. The voice at the end — that low, near-familiar “Oh, really now?” — a dream. The dead do not speak. That is the whole point of what I did. That is the whole point of everything.

(He is gone. I made sure of it. What I am hearing is guilt producing sounds. Guilt is not a ghost. It cannot hurt me.)

I lay back down and waited for morning, and morning came the way it always did in this world: warm, unhurried, with the smell of food rising from below like something designed to make you feel safe.

The days settled into a rhythm. Mother cooked. Father asked about school. Dinner was a conversation, not a performance of one. I was careful. I was precise. Every morning I told myself: you planned this. You are good at this. Stay sharp.

About a week in, I came downstairs to find both my parents on the couch with an old photo album open across their laps. Father was pointing. Mother was already laughing before she saw me.

Mom: “Come look. This is you at six.”

A small boy covered in mud, grinning impossibly wide. I sat down and looked at the photo and kept my face arranged into the right shape.

Dad: “You fell in the ditch trying to catch a frog and came home completely serious, telling us the frog had cheated. You were so offended.”

They both laughed. I laughed one beat after them, which is one beat too late, but not so late that anyone counts.

(That child is not me. That mud is not mine. That grin belongs to a boy who grew up in this house and learned to walk in these halls and called these two people Mom and Dad for his entire life, and I poisoned him over a meal and watched him die and then moved into his room. And now I am sitting on his couch laughing at his childhood like I earned the right.)

Mom: “And this one — first day of school. You wouldn’t let go of my hand.”

Me: “I’ve forgotten most of it, honestly. It’s been so long.”

Dad: “That’s okay. You’ve grown up so much. My little frog-chaser.”

He said it with so much uncomplicated love that something behind my sternum moved in a way I had not anticipated and could not fully name. He was aiming all of that at a grave. He just did not know it yet.

(Stop. Stop that. This is the life I chose. These are the terms.)

The friends were harder. Old Friend A and Old Friend B had a shorthand with him built from years of shared stupid moments — inside references and half-finished sentences that landed perfectly between them and reached me like radio signals from a station I couldn’t tune into. I sat with them at the bench behind the building and kept my responses one beat behind and my smiles half a second late and hoped they attributed it to “maturing.”

Old Friend B: “You’re different lately. Like, a different kind of quiet.”

Me: “People change.”

Old Friend A: “Sure, but you changed all at once. Like someone flipped a switch.”

(Closer than you know.)

Me: “I just decided to be more serious. That’s all.”

They accepted this because they wanted to. People tend to choose the interpretation that requires the least amount of grief. I was counting on that.

But the mistakes accumulated. I answered a question about his childhood hobby with the wrong hobby. I flinched at a sound that, apparently, had never bothered him before. I forgot which side of the bed he slept on and mother noticed the pillow was wrong. Small things. Each one individually dismissible. Together they were beginning to form a shape.

At dinner, mother watched me eat something and set her fork down.

Mom: “You used to hate this. You’d make a whole face about it.”

Me: “I guess my taste changed.”

She looked at father. Father looked back. That quiet, wordless exchange between people who have been married long enough to hold an entire conversation in a glance. Then they both smiled and moved on. They had already decided on the explanation — he’s changing, he’s grown up — and they were going to keep choosing it as long as I didn’t give them a reason not to.

(Good. Let them rationalize. As long as they rationalize.)

I said goodnight and went upstairs and turned the light off and lay in the dark. And the dark was just the dark. No voice. No presence. Just a room and a boy in it who had killed someone to be there.

The guilt arrived then, not dramatically, just quietly, like water finding the lowest point in a room. I let it settle. It didn’t feel like punishment yet. It just felt like weight.

(He’s gone. He can’t say anything. He can’t feel anything. The voice was a dream and this is just guilt and guilt is manageable. Guilt is just the price of ambition.)

I closed my eyes.

From somewhere very close — not outside, not in the hall, not in any space I could point to — a sensation. Like being looked at from inside.

I convinced myself it was nothing. I was very convincing.

Chapter Three

The Shape of Absence

The group project was when I understood the true scale of what I could not take.

Grades: taken. Schedule: taken. Face, name, room, parents, history: taken. But the way he existed between people — the specific voltage of him in a social space, the way his presence made rooms feel louder without him saying anything — that was not a thing that transferred. It was not stored anywhere I could access. It had simply ended with him.

Old Friend A made a half-finished joke. The kind where the punchline is unspoken because the other person is supposed to feel it before it arrives. Both of them looked at me, waiting for whatever came next. Whatever he always said in that slot.

I said something technically correct. The laughter it got was polite. The silence after was brief but total.

(I have his face. I have his grades. I do not have whatever made people lean toward him. That part didn’t come through the mirror. That part stayed with the body.)

Old Friend B held back after the session ended.

Old Friend B: “I’m not trying to be weird about this. I just — are you actually okay? Because you feel like you’re somewhere else even when you’re right here.”

The specific care in his voice — the genuine worry aimed at a person who was no longer in the room to receive it — did something to the inside of my throat. I had not planned for this particular texture of guilt. The kind that comes not from what you took but from who is still looking for it.

Me: “I’m fine. Really.”

He looked at me for a long moment. Then nodded. Then left.

On the walk home I let the thought go all the way to the end for the first time, not stopping it where it became uncomfortable.

(I cannot be him. Not fully. Not in the ways that matter to people who loved him. I can maintain the surface. I can keep the grades and the schedule and the polite smile at dinner. But every time someone who knew him reaches toward who he was, I will not be there. There will be nothing there but the performance of someone trying to be there. And people can feel that gap even when they can’t name it. Old Friend B can feel it. The family feels it. They just keep choosing the explanation they can live with.)

(And there is nothing I can do about any of it. I killed the only person who could have filled that space.)

That was the thought that sat down in me and didn’t move. Not abstract guilt. The specific, surgical understanding that what I destroyed was irreplaceable, and I had known it was irreplaceable, and I had done it anyway.

That night I lay in bed and spoke to the ceiling.

Me: “I’m sorry.”

The silence answered the way silence always does: completely, and without mercy.

But then — under the silence, or inside it, or in the part of me that the silence reached — something shifted. Something that was not me registered the apology. Not with words. With something older than words. A sensation like pressure behind the eyes. Like being recognized.

I sat up in bed.

The room was empty. Of course it was empty. Of course.

Chapter Four

The First Error

I was studying for an exam. Four pages of notes, clean and organized, written in my careful hand. I knew the material. I always knew the material. I closed the notebook and went downstairs for dinner and came back and opened it again for a final review.

Near the bottom of the third page, there was a sentence.

I had not written it.

I knew with complete certainty that I had not written it, the way you know the inside of your own hand. The letters were close to mine — close enough that someone else might not have noticed — but the angle was wrong. The pressure was different. The loops on certain letters did not curve the way mine do. And the content of it, the cold, slightly contemptuous precision of it, was not mine either.

You skipped a step in the derivation. Line 4. Also: I can tell when you’re pretending not to feel things. You’re doing it right now.

I checked line 4. There was an error. A real one, the kind I would have caught on second reading. The sentence had caught it instead.

I sat very still with the notebook in my hands and catalogued every rational explanation available to me. Dissociation. Stress. Unconscious self-correction that I didn’t register writing. The handwriting difference was explainable — angle, fatigue, the pen held differently.

The second half of the sentence was not explainable. I can tell when you’re pretending not to feel things. That was not a study note. That was addressed to me from something that had been watching me.

(He is dead. Dead people don’t observe. Dead people don’t correct homework. Dead people do not know what I am pretending.)

(Dead people do not have opinions about what I feel.)

I corrected line 4. I closed the notebook. I went to bed and told myself what I always told myself when reality presented something I didn’t have a place for: it’s stress, it’s tiredness, there’s an explanation, I just don’t have it yet.

In the morning the sentence was still there. Exactly as I had left it. The handwriting had not changed back overnight the way I had half-hoped it might.

I sat at the desk and wrote my own name in my own hand and compared the two. They were not the same hand.

(Okay.)

(Okay. I’ll figure this out. I always figure things out.)

I said it until I believed it, which took longer than usual. Then I got dressed and went to school and performed a normal day with the thoroughness of someone who understood that performance was now load-bearing.

On the way home, for the first time, I felt watched from inside my own body.

Chapter Five

Echoes

The handwriting came back. Not every day. Enough.

In the margins of my notes: corrections, additions, observations. Always accurate. Always in that hand that was almost mine but colder, more angular, as though written by someone who had learned my penmanship from watching rather than from being me. The content shifted over time. At first it was only academic. Then it became something else.

You flinched when father touched your shoulder. He noticed. He didn’t say anything because he’s kind. That’s the difference between us. He was always the one people wanted to protect.

You answered Old Friend A wrong today. The real answer was funnier. He would have known that. You don’t know that.

I’m not haunting you. I want to be clear about that. I don’t know what this is either. But I am here and you know I’m here and I think that’s going to be a problem for you.

I started the journal on a Thursday. A separate notebook, bought with my own money, kept in the back of the desk drawer. Date, time, observation. I was going to document this methodically. I was going to find the mechanism and address it. I was not going to let something I didn’t understand yet become something that undid me.

The teacher incident happened the following Monday.

I was mid-answer in class when he held up his hand.

Teacher: “You already told me this. Yesterday. After class. You came to my desk and explained this exact sequence for almost ten minutes.”

I had not stayed after class. I had gone directly home, eaten dinner, studied, gone to bed. My memory of the afternoon was complete and unambiguous.

Me: “I think there might be a misunderstanding, sir.”

Teacher: “I remember it very clearly.”

The classroom was quiet. Twenty faces waiting. I kept my expression neutral because the alternative was letting the thing behind my face show, and I was not yet ready to name what the thing behind my face was.

(Something was at that desk yesterday. In my body, with my voice, explaining things I would have explained exactly that way. And I was not there for it. I was somewhere else. Which means for some portion of yesterday afternoon, I was not the one driving.)

Me: “I apologize for the confusion, sir.”

Then Old Friend A was mid-sentence about something and I answered the question before he finished it. Clear and correct. As though I had heard it before. Because some part of me had — some part of me that listened to things before I did, that watched what was coming before I could see it.

Old Friend A: “How did you know what I was going to say?”

Me: “Lucky guess.”

That night in the journal, I wrote for a long time. Then I read the last entry back and the handwriting on the final two lines was not mine.

You should be more scared than you are. I’m not saying that to be cruel. I’m saying it because the fact that you’re not scared enough is probably going to cost you.

I stared at those two lines for a very long time. Then I wrote in my own hand directly beneath them:

What do you want.

I waited.

No answer came that night. But in the morning, the journal had one new line.

That’s a better question than “what are you.” I’ll give you that.

He was learning how to talk to me. That was the part that kept me awake that night.

Chapter Six

The Reflection Delay

Saturday morning. Half awake. The bathroom. Brush teeth, look up.

My hand was down. Mouth closed. Arm at my side.

In the mirror, the reflection was still brushing.

Three full seconds of it — slow, deliberate circles, completely unaware that I had stopped. Then it caught up. Hand down. Perfectly synchronized. Looking back at me with my own eyes, exactly as it always had.

I stood completely still and looked at my own face in the glass and tried to determine which of us was doing the looking.

(Visual lag. Sleep deprivation. The brain processing input in the wrong order. There is a documented phenomenon. This is that phenomenon.)

I rinsed and left and did not look back.

Three days later I was getting dressed. I reached for my collar. And in the mirror, the reflection turned its head.

Before I did.

Not a lag. An anticipation. It knew I was going to turn and it turned first, as though it were making a decision rather than reflecting one. I froze. It froze too. We stood looking at each other and the image in the glass was perfectly still and perfectly normal and I could not stop seeing the moment before it, the fraction of a second where the reflection acted and I followed.

(He is in there. He is in the glass because he is in me and the reflection is reflecting both of us and sometimes he gets there first. He doesn’t speak yet. He just… moves. Sometimes before I do.)

I bought a cloth and covered the bedroom mirror that evening. Mother asked why.

Me: “The light angle gives me headaches in the morning.”

She accepted this. I was grateful for how much people will accept when the alternative requires them to worry.

I started avoiding the bathroom mirror too. I got very good at brushing teeth by feel. I developed the habit of looking at my hands while I washed them rather than up at the glass.

Every night before bed I wrote in the journal: reflection: no change.

What I meant was: it has not looked back at me with its own expression yet. It has not smiled when I wasn’t smiling. I am counting the nights it has not done this because when I stop being able to write that it will be the clearest evidence yet of how bad things have become.

But I did not write all of that. I just wrote: reflection: no change.

(He is getting stronger. I felt it when I got to the desk chair this morning — the slight resistance, like moving through water. As though my body considered two directions before choosing mine. He couldn’t hold it. But he considered it.)

I started doing things faster. Making decisions before I could feel him considering them. Preempting. Racing myself to my own body.

Chapter Seven

Memory Bleed

The dreams started and they were not nightmares. That was the worst part. They were warm.

His grandmother’s birthday. A park route he had memorized before he could read. An argument with his father over curfew where his father eventually laughed and gave in. I woke from these not with the gasping disorient of nightmares but with the slow, heavy surfacing of someone who had been somewhere real. The memories settled in my body the way lived memories do, with texture and weight and the particular kind of certainty that belongs only to things that actually happened.

By the end of the second week, I knew things I had no right to know.

I ducked slightly passing a shelf in the school library without thinking about it. The broken bracket that would have caught my sleeve was exactly where I knew it would be, because he had caught his sleeve on it a dozen times, and now so had I, in a memory I hadn’t lived. I knew which corner of the cafeteria had the bad lighting and which teacher preferred questions before class rather than after. I knew the specific pitch of Old Friend A’s laugh when something genuinely surprised him versus when he was being polite.

Mother served something at breakfast and I ate it without comment.

Mom: “You used to make a face every time I made this. You called it ‘punishment food.’”

Me: “I don’t remember that.”

And I genuinely did not. Whatever visceral aversion he had built for it was not in the memories that arrived. I had received the route and the library shelf and the friends’ laughs, but not the tastes and the textures, not the embodied things. His memories came without his body’s reactions. They were his mind without his nerves. His map without his feet.

One afternoon at the bench, Old Friend A started complaining and I said the exact response before I consciously planned it. The right timing, the right delivery, the specific joke that landed. Old Friend A went quiet for a second and then laughed — a real laugh, the surprised kind.

Old Friend A: “You haven’t said that in months. I thought you retired it.”

(I didn’t say it. He said it. Through me. I was the medium and he was the message and it lasted about two seconds and neither of them noticed the seam where he ended and I began because there was no seam. Not anymore. Not in those two seconds.)

That night I wrote in the journal and the entry was mine until the last paragraph, where the handwriting shifted.

You used my joke. I’m not angry. I just want you to understand what that means. The things you know that you shouldn’t — routes, habits, punch lines — those are mine. You didn’t earn them. You absorbed them. And you’re going to keep absorbing them. This is what ‘sharing a body’ actually means. Not just the space. Everything.

I read that three times. Then I wrote beneath it:

Does that mean I’m losing myself? Or gaining you?

The answer came in the morning.

Yes.

One word. No elaboration. As though the question was not complicated enough to require one.

Chapter Eight

Compression

I sat at the desk for forty minutes before I picked up the pen. This was not reluctance. It was the specific dread of writing something that, once written, cannot be unwritten. Once I put it on the page I owned it. Once I owned it I had to act on it.

I picked up the pen.

He is not haunting me. Haunting is something that happens from outside. What is happening is inside. He didn’t go away when his body died. He came with me through the mirror. He is in here. We are in the same body, in the same space, and the space is his original space, which means I am the one who doesn’t fit. I removed him from his life. I did not remove him from himself. I just removed the space between us. We are the same location now. One body. Two people. And one of us is getting stronger.

I put the pen down. Read what I’d written. The handwriting was entirely mine. No intrusions this time. He had let me write it myself because he wanted me to own it. That was the kind of cruelty I was beginning to recognize as his style — quiet, patient, surgical.

The impulses had been building for weeks. I would reach for something and feel a second opinion about which hand to use. I would form a sentence and feel an alternative version rising beneath it. They were not voices. There was no voice. It was more like being a river with a current running through it that was not quite the same current. Sometimes the currents aligned. Sometimes one pushed against the other and you could feel the friction without being able to see it.

(I am not losing my mind. I know exactly what is happening. What is happening is that there are two of us in here and we are not compatible and neither of us can leave.)

I wrote one more line in the journal.

I didn’t remove him. I removed the space between us. And I don’t know what happens next to either of us.

The handwriting on the word either shifted, almost imperceptibly, before shifting back.

He was reading along. He always read along.

(I know you’re reading this.)

I didn’t write that in the journal. I thought it very clearly and directly, aimed inward, the way you aim a thought at a person in the room with you. A beat of silence. Then the faintest sensation of being acknowledged.

Not words. But not nothing either.

The first time we communicated directly. Neither of us chose it. Neither of us was ready.

Chapter Nine

Internal Battle: Part 1

Once he knew that I knew, the pretense ended.

The handwriting in the margins stopped. The subtle pushes against my decisions stopped. What replaced them was direct, immediate, and considerably harder to manage.

He tried to take the body.

The first time was in the morning. I was reaching for my school bag and the arm moved wrong — not toward the bag but toward the desk, deliberate and purposeful, going somewhere I hadn’t decided to go. I pulled back. There was a moment of genuine resistance, two intentions occupying the same limb, the physical sensation of it like trying to open a door that is also being pushed shut from the other side. Then my direction won. The arm went to the bag.

I stood in the middle of the room breathing hard.

(He’s trying to take control. Actually trying. Not just influencing. Not just writing in margins. He wants the body.)

He didn’t get it. Not for lack of trying but because the body wasn’t his anymore. He could get a limb. He could get a few seconds. But he couldn’t hold it.

Not yet.

The attempts kept coming. On the way to school: the legs wanted to turn left when I turned right, and I felt the disagreement in my knees. During class: my hand began writing something I hadn’t thought of, and I pressed the pen hard against the paper to reassert. During dinner: my mouth almost said something that would have confused my parents badly, and I caught it just in time and took a long drink of water.

The strain of it was exhausting in a way I had no framework for. This was not the exhaustion of effort or of stress. It was the exhaustion of having to consciously inhabit yourself. Of never, at any point, being able to simply be without checking that the self doing the being was you.

Dinner was harder than anything else. The family asked questions — ordinary ones, the conversational texture of an evening meal — and I had to answer them with part of my attention while the rest of my attention held the body’s controls, felt for any drift, corrected for any push. I was monitoring two things simultaneously: the performance of a son at the table, and the quiet war beneath it. Father made a joke. I laughed exactly one beat late. Mother watched my face for half a second longer than usual.

(She noticed. She’ll attribute it to tiredness. She usually does. I need to be better tomorrow. I need to be more present. But being more present requires attention I am currently spending on not letting someone else use my hands at the dinner table.)

The mental toll compounded. By the end of the week I was making errors in class. Small ones, uncharacteristic. My concentration fractured at unpredictable moments. I arrived at the ends of conversations with no memory of the middles, which was visible in ways I couldn’t fully manage.

(He’s wearing me down. He can’t take the body directly but he’s weakening it. Every failed attempt costs both of us something and he knows I can’t afford to keep paying.)

I started sleeping less. Specifically to stay sharp. Specifically to prevent him from using the gap of unconsciousness to do things I wouldn’t know about. This was not a sustainable strategy, which I knew, but sustainability had become a secondary concern.

The necklace was still on the desk, where it had been since the night I thought I’d destroyed it and found I hadn’t. I had put a book on top of it. I had not moved the book. But I was aware of it the way you are aware of a bruise — not constantly, but whenever something brushed near it.

One evening after dinner I sat at the desk and thought at him, directly, the way I had learned to:

(You can’t hold it. It cuts off every time. You know that.)

The answer came as a sensation rather than words. Something between amusement and patience. The feeling of someone who has decided to wait you out because they know something you don’t, and they are comfortable in the knowing, and they have decided that letting you figure it out yourself will be instructive.

I did not sleep well that night. He did not need sleep. That was the first asymmetry I had not accounted for.

Chapter Ten

Internal Battle: Part 2

Two weeks into the open war, he found the method.

It was not strength. It was subtlety. He could not overpower me when I was alert and rested and fully engaged with the act of being here. But when I was stressed — genuinely stressed, the kind that made everything loosen its grip slightly — the resistance dropped. The door I held shut by vigilance cracked open. And he was through it before I could close it again.

The bad day that gave him his first real foothold was ordinary in its badness. An exam result lower than expected. An interaction with Old Friend B that I fumbled in a way I couldn’t recover from — he had said something searching and I had said something closed in return, and I had seen his face adjust, the way people’s faces adjust when they have been reaching for something and found it wasn’t there. A comment from father at dinner that was kind but landed somewhere tender. By the time I got to my room I was frayed in a way that showed in the body’s grip on itself.

I sat down at the desk to write in the journal. The next thing I was aware of was standing at the window in the dark with my hand against the glass, looking at the street below, with no memory of getting up.

He had had the body for approximately forty minutes.

I checked the journal. There was an entry in his handwriting:

HimForty-three minutes. I didn’t do anything with it. I just wanted you to know I could. I walked to the window. I looked at the street. I thought about how it used to be mine. How I used to look at this same street from this same window and it was just — mine. No maintenance required. No war. Just mine. I wanted to feel what that felt like again. Even for forty-three minutes. I think you understand that, actually. Better than you’d like to.

I read that three times. Then I wrote beneath it:

If you do anything that makes the family suspicious I will find a way to make this worse for you.

His response came in the morning, while I was getting dressed and trying not to look at the covered mirror:

HimYou can’t make things worse for me. I’m already dead and sharing a body with my killer. You, on the other hand, have a family downstairs eating breakfast. You have things to lose. I don’t. Keep that asymmetry in mind when you’re deciding how to talk to me.

He was right. That was the infuriating part. He had nothing left to protect. He was already past every threshold of loss. I was the one maintaining a performance, a cover, a life that required his cooperation or at least his non-interference to continue. My vulnerability was my investment in the outcome.

I started managing my emotional state the way you manage an injury in a location you cannot stop using. Carefully. Constantly. Limiting the situations that could compromise it. I slept at consistent times. I ate well. I avoided situations with unpredictable emotional weight. I became, in the pursuit of self-preservation, a very well-regulated person.

The family noticed this too, but differently from before. Before, the inconsistencies had been of personality — the wrong reaction, the wrong preference. Now they were of availability. I was present at the table but not fully present. I answered correctly but slightly late. I smiled at the right things but the smile didn’t go all the way in.

Mother, one evening: “Are you sleeping well? You seem tired in a way that sleep doesn’t fix.”

Me: “I’m fine. School is just busy.”

She accepted this. She was very good at accepting things. But I noticed she refilled my tea without being asked, and she watched me over the rim of her cup in the way parents watch when they have decided not to push but have not decided not to worry.

(I am losing the dinner table. Not because anyone found out. Because managing him takes the part of me that used to be relaxed at dinner, and relaxed is what the table needs, and now I bring something different and they can feel the difference even when they can’t name it.)

He adapted too. Without the stress trigger being as available, he found smaller gaps. Moments of genuine distraction. Moments when my attention was fully occupied elsewhere and the grip on the body loosened by default. He used them not to act dramatically but to establish presence. A sentence inserted into a classroom discussion that I hadn’t planned. A detour on the walk home that I became aware of only when the street was wrong. Small enough that each instance was explainable. Together, they were building something.

Father asked one morning if I remembered a conversation from the previous evening. I had no memory of it. I said I must have been half-asleep, which was the kind of explanation that works once. Mother mentioned I had said something strange to her about the garden — something fond and specific, about a plant she’d had since before he was born — and she had been pleased by it and a little surprised. I had not been in the kitchen when this apparently happened.

He was talking to them. When he had the body, he wasn’t wasting it on gestures. He was talking to my family, in my voice, building a version of me in their memories that I couldn’t fully see or track or correct for. A version that knew about the garden. That had the right warmth at the right moments. That was, perhaps, more like the person they remembered than I had managed to be.

(He’s not trying to expose me. He’s trying to reclaim them. He’s trying to be their son again, in whatever increments are available to him. And the increments are working. The look on mother’s face when she told me about the garden comment. That look was not meant for me.)

I wrote in the journal that night:

You’re trying to surface. To exist to them in the spaces I leave. To be present to them in ways I can’t manage anymore.

His answer came the next morning, and it was one line, and it was the line I least wanted to read:

HimI’m trying to go home. You of all people should understand that.

I sat with that for a long time. The journal open on the desk. The house quiet around me. His answer sitting on the page in his handwriting, which looked more like mine every week, which was its own particular horror.

I understood exactly. That was the problem.

Chapter Eleven

The Other

Old Friend B found me at the bench on a Thursday.

Not at lunch — after school, which was unusual. He sat down without asking if it was okay, which meant he had been thinking about this for long enough that he’d moved past the stage of asking.

Old Friend B: “I want to ask you something and I want you to actually answer it. Not the answer you give when you’re trying to make someone stop worrying.”

Me: “Okay.”

Old Friend B: “What happened to you? Not what’s going on with school, not stress, not growing up. What actually happened. Because something happened. Something changed in you all at once and I can see it and it’s been months and it’s not getting more normal, it’s getting less normal, and you keep giving me answers that are shaped like answers but don’t actually contain anything. And I would like to know.”

He looked at me with the specific steady attention of someone who has decided to wait as long as it takes. No impatience. No performance of concern. Just the real thing, aimed directly at me, and the real thing was harder to deflect than the performed version would have been.

(He is asking me to tell him the truth. The actual truth. He is asking in a way that makes it clear he will accept a difficult answer, that he is prepared for one, that the asking is an act of care so genuine it has weight. And I have nothing to give him. I have a cover story he can already see through and a secret that would end every relationship in this house and a war inside my own body that I cannot explain to anyone.)

(I want to tell him. That is the part I cannot afford to look at directly. Somewhere under the management and the vigilance and the performance, I want to sit on this bench and say: something happened to me that I cannot undo and I am not the person you’re looking for and I am so tired of pretending I am.)

Me: “I don’t know how to explain it. It’s… something shifted. I’m trying to work through it. I know it shows. I’m sorry it shows.”

He looked at me for a long moment. He was deciding something.

Old Friend B: “Okay. I’m not going to push. But I need you to know that I’m still here. Whatever it is. You don’t have to tell me what happened. But you can. If you ever want to. That option is open.”

He got up and left. I sat on the bench for a while after he was gone. The afternoon was the specific quality of late afternoon that makes everything feel slightly more permanent than it is.

(I chose this life because I wanted people in it. A family. Belonging. People who would notice if something was wrong with me. People who would offer to help. And now I have those people and I cannot use them and the having of them has become its own particular form of isolation because every act of genuine care they offer me is something I have to deflect instead of receive. The thing I killed someone for is the thing I am prevented from having. That is the specific shape of it. I have not let myself see that shape clearly until right now.)

I walked home. Inside me, he was quiet. He had been listening to the whole conversation from wherever he lived in the body’s interior, and now he was quiet with the quality of someone who has heard something they recognize and are not sure what to do with.

HimHe was my friend first. Just so you know. He would have done the same for me.

(I know.)

HimHe deserves a better answer than the one you gave him.

(I know that too.)

Neither of us said anything after that. We just walked the rest of the way home in an unusual silence — not the silence of a war suspended, but the silence of two people who have been reminded of something they share that is not the conflict.

• • •

We were both in the body when the other version appeared. That was becoming more normal — both present, neither fully dominant, a kind of tense shared occupancy that cost me energy and, I suspected, frustrated him enormously. The walk home. The usual route. The street with the broken tile.

He was standing across the road.

My face. My height. My build. Completely still on the opposite pavement, looking directly at us as though he had been waiting there for some time and had already decided not to run.

I stopped. Inside me, he stopped too — the simultaneous arrest, both of us recognizing the same impossible thing at the same moment, neither of us having a word for it.

The figure across the street was afraid. Not threatened. Not aggressive. Afraid with the settled quality of someone who has been afraid for so long it has become structural — built into the posture, into the way he held his shoulders already braced, into the eyes that were very still in a face that was trying not to show anything.

And then he walked away. Quickly. Head down.

We stood at the corner for a moment. Then I started walking. He didn’t push for control. He was too occupied with what we had both just seen.

That night the journal entry was mine until it wasn’t:

There is another one. My face. He was afraid. He is in the same position you were — someone crossed over into his world and took his life. Another artifact. Another crossing. The necklace said nothing about where displaced people go. I assumed they just — stopped. Were absorbed somewhere. Ceased. I did not think about what it would mean for them to land somewhere real and keep existing in it.

His handwriting cut into the paragraph:

HimI didn’t know either. I thought the same thing you thought — that displacement was clean. That the original just went somewhere unspecific and that was the end of it. The necklace let me believe that because I wanted to believe it. I think that is the thing about the artifact that neither of us understood until now: it doesn’t lie. It just doesn’t volunteer the parts you would prefer not to know.

We sat with that together — which was a strange thing to experience, sitting with something together. Two people in one body, sharing the same silence, both looking at the same wall, both turning over the same understanding. Not at peace. Not comfortable. But for one specific moment, genuinely in the same situation with the same information and the same unresolved feeling about what the information meant.

(For what it’s worth. I didn’t know displacing you would send you somewhere. I thought you’d just stop. I was wrong. I’m not sure that changes anything. But I was wrong about that specific part.)

A beat. Then, from him, in the space between thoughts rather than in the journal:

HimI know. That doesn’t change what you did. But I know.

The most honest exchange we had ever had. Neither of us had chosen it. It had arrived because something outside us was bigger than the war between us, and for one moment the war had simply — paused.

Chapter Twelve

Lots of Me

There was another one on Wednesday. Two more on Friday. By the following week I had counted seven.

They appeared in ordinary places at ordinary times, which made it worse. In the school corridor, briefly, before disappearing around a corner that led nowhere. At the edge of the park. On the far side of the street outside the convenience store. Always the same: my face, my height, my build, wearing different clothes, carrying themselves with different postures. Each one distinct. Each one a separate person who happened to be wearing my face on a body that had arrived here from somewhere else.

Only we could see them. I knew this because I watched other people’s eyes carefully and nobody tracked them. A version of me could stand directly in the path of a student walking down the hall and the student would not flinch, would not adjust their route, would walk through the space the figure occupied without noticing. The version would not displace. It was more like — both things occupied the space and the ordinary world only registered one of them.

HimThey can’t see each other either. I watched the one near the convenience store. He was looking right at the one near the park and registering nothing. It’s only us. Only from inside this body, from this specific position, that they’re visible.

(Why? What is it about our position that makes them visible to us and not to anyone else?)

HimI think it’s the necklace. Even destroyed, the crossing leaves something. We’re sensitized to versions in a way that regular people aren’t. We can see the seams.

I started mapping them in the journal. Dates, times, locations, distinguishing features. The one with the different gait. The one who looked older in a way that had nothing to do with age. The one who kept looking over his shoulder, as though already aware of being watched, though not by us. By something else.

One of them looked directly at me. Only once. His gaze found me across a crowded street and held for a half-second before he looked away and kept walking. No recognition. No acknowledgment. He could not see me looking back. But the direction of his glance had been exact.

(They’re not aware of us. But something about what we are registers on them the way a sound registers on an animal even when the animal can’t locate the source. We are the anomaly in the space and they can feel the anomaly without seeing it.)

The number kept growing. Two more the following day. Three the day after. Different ones each time — I could tell because the clothes changed, the postures changed, the particular quality of their fear changed. Some of them looked like they had been afraid for a very long time. Some of them looked newly afraid, still in the early stage when fear is sharp rather than structural.

The world was fracturing in a specific way. A way that only we had the angle to see.

And the fracturing was accelerating.

Chapter Thirteen

Crack in Reality

On a Thursday morning, walking to school, I saw one of them and reached out instinctively — a reflex, not a decision — and my hand passed through the space he occupied. Cold. Like reaching through a gap in a wall to the outside air. The version didn’t react. My hand came back and felt normal and I kept walking.

The crack appeared the following Monday.

It looked exactly like what the word implies: a fracture in the visible surface of things, running from somewhere above my eye level down to the ground, fine as a hair at its edges and slightly wider in the middle. Like a mirror dropped but not yet fallen. Reality behind it looked the same as reality in front of it. But when I looked directly at the crack, I could see another version standing on its far side.

And this one was looking back.

He could see me. I could see him. Through the crack we looked at each other — same face, same eyes, same fundamental material — with the specific, strange disorientation of a mirror that shows you your reflection and your reflection shows you back and both of you know that you are not each other.

I reached toward the crack. My hand met resistance. Then something gave and my fingers were through it, into whatever space he occupied, and he took a breath and reached back and we touched. Briefly. The texture of his hand was real.

Him (inner)Talk to him. This might be important.

Me: “Do you know what’s happening?”

Other: “The cracks. Yes. I thought I was the only one who could see them.”

Me: “No. There are more of us.”

Other: “How many?”

Me: “I’ve counted at least fifteen. I think there are more.”

The crack began to narrow. I could feel it like a change in pressure — the space closing, the interface mending itself.

Other: “I don’t know how to stop it. The cracks. I’ve been trying to find a way to —”

The crack closed. My hand was back on my side, alone and cold, with nothing to show for the contact except the absolute certainty that it had been real.

The cracks kept appearing after that. More of them, in more places. Sometimes to versions of me. Sometimes, increasingly, to versions of other people — the professor, mid-lecture, with a crack running across the whiteboard behind him and his double standing there, saying the opposite of everything he said:

Professor: “The data supports the conclusion.”

Double, through the crack: “The data is being misread. The conclusion is wrong.”

Professor: “I expect you to have this section completed by Friday.”

Double, through the crack: “Push back. This deadline is unreasonable given the material.”

Only I could hear the double. Only I could see the crack. And they were telling me contradictory things, one through ordinary air and one through a fracture in the world, and both of them completely serious, and I stood in front of the professor trying to answer a question while managing the noise of two versions of him arguing about the correct answer.

I excused myself from the conversation with a coherent sentence that I composed using approximately thirty percent of my available attention. The rest was managing the crack until it closed.

(This is getting worse. The cracks are more frequent. They’re to other people now, not just to versions of me. The world is splitting along its seams and I am the only one who can see the seams splitting.)

HimWe caused this. Or rather — the crossing caused this. The necklace punched a hole in whatever keeps realities separate. We sealed it when we came through, but not perfectly. And now it’s widening.

We had broken something bigger than either of us had understood.

Chapter Fourteen

Reality on the Verge of Collapse

I was watching a version of myself through a crack when it happened.

The version was sitting on a bench in the park, looking at his hands. A wide crack had been open between us for nearly two minutes — the longest yet — and we had been almost able to speak. I was leaning toward it, listening for whatever he was trying to say, when another version stepped up behind him.

I had no warning. The standing version produced something — I couldn’t see what, the crack distorted the peripheral detail — and the sitting version was gone. Instantly. No process. Just: there, and then not there.

What happened next I cannot fully describe because I had no reference for it. The sitting version didn’t fall. He didn’t disappear into absence. He shattered — not physically, not with sound — in the way a mirror shatters when it hits the floor, a sudden fanning outward of fracture lines from a central point. And then the crack mended. Instantly. The fracture in reality sealed shut like a wound healing at impossible speed, and the standing version was gone too, and the park bench was empty on both sides.

I stood in the park alone — visibly alone, to anyone watching — with the absolute silence of something significant having just occurred.

(When a version of me dies, the crack closes. It seals completely. Faster and cleaner than any natural closing I’ve seen. The death mends the fracture.)

HimDon’t jump to conclusions. One instance is nothing. You don’t have data.

(I know. I’m not concluding. I’m observing.)

But the observation sat in me with the specific density of a thing that was going to matter later.

A week later it happened with a double of someone else. A crack opened to a version of a classmate, and a separate figure — unclear, distorted — moved through the crack, and the version was gone. This time the crack did not fully close. It narrowed and partially sealed and left a hairline fracture that I could still see, faintly, in the corner of the classroom for the rest of the day.

(My hypothesis is correct. Only my doubles’ deaths close the cracks completely. The deaths of others’ doubles close them only partially.)

I did not share this with him yet. He would have a response to it. He always had a response. I needed to think about it before that conversation happened.

Then the necklace reappeared.

I had destroyed it. I was certain of that. I had felt it come apart, had watched the pieces scatter. But on a Tuesday evening it was on the desk, completely intact, as though it had simply been waiting for the right moment to come back. The metal was warm to the touch. As though it had been held recently, by someone whose body temperature was exactly mine.

HimI didn’t put it there. I want to be clear about that.

(Then something else did. Something that wanted us to have it.)

I didn’t touch it. I put a book on top of it and went to bed and lay in the dark with the particular anxiety of a problem that has added a new variable before you’ve solved for the original ones.

But I could feel it through the cover of the book. Not physically. The way you can feel a significant object in a room even when you can’t see it. It was patient. It had always been patient. It could wait.

Chapter Fifteen

Restoration

I left it on the desk for three days. I worked around it. I moved the book. I looked at it. I did not touch it. Every morning I told myself I would decide today, and every morning I decided nothing, and the necklace sat there warm and patient and uninvested in my timeline.

The cracks were worsening. Longer open times. More frequent occurrences. Two in a single afternoon on Thursday. The last one that day had been open long enough that I could hear something on the other side that wasn’t words — a low, structural sound, like a building settling but amplified, like everything fundamental about the way things held together was under a pressure it had not been designed for.

On the fourth day I picked up the necklace.

We tried everything obvious: holding it, concentrating on it, speaking to it, attempting to replicate the conditions of the original activation. Nothing. It was warm and present and completely inert. After half an hour I was frustrated in the specific grinding way of someone who has tried and failed at something they need to succeed at. I squeezed it hard. The edge of the clasp cut my palm.

The blood was absorbed before it reached the desk. Not absorbed by the surface — absorbed by the necklace. Drunk from my skin. The metal changed under my hand, a shift of temperature and density that was unmistakable, and then it was glowing. Not visibly, not in a way anyone else in the room would have seen. Internally. The glow was inside it, reaching upward through the palm that held it.

And then it showed us.

The vision was not directed at either of us specifically — it unfolded for both, shared, seen from the same angle. Scene after scene. Each one the same structure: a crack in reality, open and widening. A figure with our face on one side of it. And a second figure, also with our face, moving toward the first.

Each scene ended the same way. The first figure gone. The crack sealed.

Scene after scene. The pattern identical. The method varying. The conclusion always the same: one version dead, one crack closed.

Then a scene different from the rest. Not in the middle of the sequence but near it, and not announced as different — just different, if you were watching closely enough. The figure approaching the crack did not continue toward the other version. It turned. It looked at the crack itself. And then it stepped through — not toward the other version on the far side but into the crack, into the fracture, into whatever was between. And the crack sealed behind it. And in the aftermath of that scene, the other cracks in the vision — the ones in the background, the distant fractures — dimmed. Not closed. Dimmed. As though the pressure on the whole system had dropped.

The necklace went dark. My palm was still bleeding. We sat in the silence of what we had been shown.

HimDid you see the middle scene?

(Yes.)

HimSomeone stepped into the crack. Not through it. Into it.

(I saw.)

HimAnd the other cracks —

(I know what it implies. I need to think.)

The room was very quiet around us. Outside, the ordinary neighborhood did its ordinary sounds. Inside, two people in one body sat with the weight of having been shown the shape of the problem, and the shape of the solutions, and what the solutions cost.

My hypothesis about the deaths was confirmed. And something else had been confirmed alongside it that neither of us was ready to speak yet.

Chapter Sixteen

Internal Conflict: Remaining Morality

The artifact gave us something else that we hadn’t expected: a shared space. Brief and sensory-thin, the quality of a place assembled from available mental material rather than built for purpose. When the necklace activated, the shared space opened — somewhere between dreaming and not, where we could see each other directly for the first time.

We were both dim shapes in the space, lit from no identifiable source. He looked like me in the way that two flames from the same match look like each other — the same essential nature, differently expressed. The brightness of both of us was the same. Equal, measured against whatever scale the artifact was using. I registered that and filed it because it didn’t fit any of my assumptions and I didn’t have time to revise them right now.

Him: “The deaths of our doubles close the cracks. You’ve known this for weeks. You didn’t tell me.”

Me: “I needed to think about it first.”

Him: “You needed to decide how to tell me in a way that made me less likely to act on it immediately.”

He was not wrong. I didn’t argue it.

Him: “The solution is clear. The doubles are already displaced. They’re versions of us that exist outside any proper world. Killing them closes the cracks. The cracks are destroying reality. We kill the doubles, we stop the destruction.”

Me: “They are people. Displaced people, yes. Afraid people. But they are not objects. They have —”

Him: “I know what they have. I know better than you do. I was displaced. I am still displaced. I know exactly what it is to be the version that got thrown out when someone else moved in.”

The silence after that was specific and uncomfortable.

Me: “Then you should understand why I won’t do it.”

Him: “I understand why you feel that way. I don’t think your feelings are wrong. I think they’re a luxury we can’t afford.”

Me: “I killed you. I have carried that since the day I got here. I am not doing it again. Not with seventeen faces that look like mine. Not with more. I will find another way.”

Him: “What other way? The necklace showed us three options. Two involve killing. One involves stepping into the crack yourself. Those are the options.”

Me: “Then I’ll find a fourth.”

The shared space held us both for a moment. His light was steady. Mine was not.

Him: “You don’t have the time to find a fourth. Look outside. Count the cracks. They are multiplying while we are having this conversation.”

I didn’t answer because he was right and I knew it and that knowledge sat on me with a weight that had nothing to do with the abstract.

The space closed. We were back in the room. The necklace was cold in my hand.

Neither of us slept that night. We lay in the dark, two people in one body, each turning the same problem over in silence. For once, the war between us was suspended. We were too busy with the larger war to bother with each other.

One week of the stalemate. The cracks worsened. Longer, more frequent, reaching into more areas of daily life. The professor incident repeated. A version of mother appeared briefly through a crack in the kitchen wall — not afraid, just confused, looking at her own hands in the specific way of someone who is not sure where she is — and closed before I could do anything.

A version of father appeared in the hallway.

I could not let it get to the family.

On the eighth day of the stalemate, he took the body while I was managing a crack in the corner of the bedroom. I didn’t notice until I was outside, and I didn’t understand until I saw the figure across the street, and I understood too late as his intention clarified in the body I shared with him like a cold current moving through warm water.

Me: No —

The crack in the air between us and the double opened. He moved through it before it closed.

I regained the body standing in an empty street. The crack was gone. The seal was complete. The familiar, specific absence of a figure that had been there.

HimOne. That’s one. Look at the crack count. It worked.

I didn’t look. I walked home with my hands shaking slightly and his satisfaction warm and unbothered somewhere in my chest that I could not expel.

He had made the decision I wouldn’t make. In my body. Using my hands. And it had worked, and I couldn’t undo it, and I knew — with a certainty that arrived like something settling into its permanent position — that this was not the last time.

Chapter Seventeen

Unexpected Consequences

He killed a second on Wednesday. A third on Friday.

I did not stop him. I told myself the first time I did not stop him that I was in shock. The second time I told myself I was conserving energy for a different approach. The third time I had no story left, so I told myself nothing and simply watched from the inside as his efficiency with the task became something I registered and refused to name.

He was clean about it. He didn’t take pleasure in it, or if he did he kept it from the parts of the shared space that reached me. He did it the way you perform a necessary and unpleasant task: with focus, with minimum theatrics, and with the specific satisfaction of a problem being solved rather than a person being ended.

After the third one, something was wrong.

It took me a moment to locate it because the wrongness was internal rather than external. Nothing in the room had changed. Nothing in the body had visibly altered. But I reached for something on the desk and the reach was slightly slower than I expected, slightly more effortful, the way your limbs feel when you’ve been sitting too long and the blood hasn’t fully returned.

I opened the shared space.

His light was marginally brighter. Mine was marginally dimmer. The difference was small enough that I might have imagined it, except that I had been paying close attention to that space since the first time the necklace activated it, and I had a baseline, and the baseline had shifted.

(Each death of a double strengthens the soul of the one who performs it. There are two souls in this body. Strength for one is proportional weakness for the other. He gets stronger every time he kills. I get weaker.)

HimI noticed it too.

Me: “You knew this would happen.”

HimI suspected. I wasn’t certain.

Me: “And you did it anyway.”

HimI did it because someone had to. You weren’t going to.

The unfairness of it was exquisite. He had acted over my objection using my body and was now stronger for it and I was weaker for it and the cracks were fewer for it and there was no version of this accounting that came out clean.

I killed three doubles in the next four days.

I want to be precise about my reasoning because precision matters here. I did it to rebalance the light. To restore myself to parity with him. To ensure that his growing strength didn’t become the only strength in the body we shared, because a body controlled entirely by him was a body that pursued his agenda without my interference, and his agenda included things I was not willing to sanction from a position of weakness.

That was the reasoning. I held it clearly and I still hold it. I also know what the reasoning cost and what it made me and I have not found a way to reconcile the two that doesn’t require me to stop thinking about it, which is not the same as resolving it.

Six doubles total in twelve days. We checked the shared space. The lights were equal again. We looked at each other across the dim interior of whatever the necklace assembled for us when it opened and neither of us said anything for a long moment.

Him: “The frequency of the cracks has dropped significantly. Six was enough to create meaningful stabilization.”

Me: “Six per week. That’s the rate. We don’t exceed it.”

Him: “You’re proposing rules now.”

Me: “I’m proposing the only constraint I can enforce. Six per week. And we alternate. You don’t get to be the only one doing it.”

He understood what I meant by that. If we shared the kills equally, neither of us could grow stronger than the other. The balance of light held. Neither of us became the body’s sole occupant by default.

Him: “And the eight hours each.”

Me: “Eight hours each. Agreed.”

Him: “… Agreed.”

The shared space closed. We were in the room. The necklace was cool on the desk. Outside, the neighborhood was its ordinary self, completely unaware that the two people in the room behind the second-floor window had just reached a formal treaty over a serial killing schedule.

Equal. For now. Both of us watching the other’s light.

Chapter Eighteen

Escalating Destruction

The six-per-week held for three months.

During those three months something approaching normalcy reasserted itself, which was its own specific horror — not the horror of chaos but the horror of a routine that should not be a routine becoming one. We alternated. We maintained the eight-hour schedule. The cracks remained present but manageable. Family life continued. School continued. The performance of a son continued, slightly more hollow now, the interior of it occupied by two people who had stopped fighting each other long enough to fight something larger.

At the end of the third month, we killed six doubles on Monday. By Thursday the cracks were back at their pre-reduction frequency. We killed two more. The cracks stabilized, but not to where they had been before — to a ceiling slightly lower than the previous one, as though the problem had noted our adjustment and filed a counter-adjustment of its own.

We held eight per week. Two months later the cracks were back. We added more. A month after that the number moved again. Three weeks later it moved again. The intervals were shrinking. Every benchmark we reached became the new floor, and the floor kept dropping, and the rate required to hold it kept climbing, and the climbing kept accelerating.

What had been weekly became twice weekly. Twice weekly became every other day. By the fifth month we were killing every day. By the end of that month, one per day held the line for approximately six hours before new cracks opened to replace the ones we had closed.

A daily massacre. That was the word that arrived one morning while I was writing in the journal, and I crossed it out immediately because naming it made it worse, and then I left it crossed out but still readable because honesty mattered in the private record. Even when honesty made the private record unbearable.

HimWe need to increase the rate again.

Me: “We increase every time and it holds for less time than the time before. You see that. You’re tracking it too.”

HimWhat else do you suggest? We stop and watch it collapse?

Me: “I’m suggesting that increasing is not a solution. It is a delay. And the delay keeps getting shorter.”

HimA shorter delay is still a delay. I’ll take it.

He wanted to escalate. He was ready to escalate. I could feel the impatience of it in the shared space — his light steady and certain while mine turned the problem over and over and kept arriving at the same answer he didn’t want to hear.

I checked the soul-light that evening. Equal, still. Both of us at the same level, both of us watching the same data lead to the same conclusion that neither of us was ready to act on.

(He wants more deaths. More kills, faster, in greater quantity. He thinks if we hit the right number we can outrun the collapse. He is wrong. The system adjusts to whatever we do. There is no number. But he is not going to accept that from reasoning alone. He needs to reach it himself.)

(And I cannot wait for him to reach it himself. Because while I am waiting, the destruction is widening. And he is going to break the treaty again the moment he decides his patience has run out. I have watched him long enough to know exactly when that moment is coming.)

(It is coming soon. Tonight, maybe. Tomorrow at the latest.)

I did not let him get there first.

During my control hours that night, I went out. I did not hold to the alternating schedule. I did not hold to any schedule. I went out and I kept going and I did not stop, and the cracks closed behind me in sequence, and the closure was clean and complete and added up faster than anything we had managed under the treaty.

I came back to the room before his control hours began. The cracks in my peripheral vision were the fewest I had seen in months. The shared space, when I opened it, showed my light noticeably brighter than his. He was still present, still lit, but the gap between us had shifted in a direction it had never shifted before.

He registered it immediately.

HimWhat did you do.

It was not a question. He already knew. He was asking me to say it.

Me: “What you were going to do. I just did it first.”

The shared space was very quiet. His light was steady but something in it had shifted in texture — not dimmer, not weaker, but different. The specific quality of someone who has been outmaneuvered by the thing they thought they understood best.

HimYou broke the agreement.

Me: “You were going to break it. I decided I preferred to be the one who broke it first.”

Him…

He had no answer. Not because he was defeated — he was not defeated, not yet, not even close — but because the argument was not wrong, and he knew it, and knowing it was its own specific frustration. I had anticipated him. I had read the timeline of his patience and acted inside it. He was the one who was supposed to be proactive. He was the one who had broken agreements before. The role had reversed without warning and he had not been prepared for that.

For the first time since he had begun pushing back, I had surprised him. I did not feel good about it. But I noted, carefully, that I did not feel as bad about it as I had expected to.

That was the part that kept me awake afterward. Not what I had done. What I had felt while doing it.

Chapter Nineteen

Battle for Dominance

He figured it out within two days.

Not through anything I told him — I had told him nothing about the soul-light shift, about the fact that my kills were accumulating as strength while his were not, about the gap that had opened between our lights while he slept through his reduced control hours. I had said none of it. I had simply acted and kept the mechanism to myself and let the results speak in ways he could not immediately interpret.

But he was not slow, and he had been watching me carefully for months, and when he opened the shared space on the second day and saw the disparity clearly for the first time, the understanding arrived on his face — even in that space, even as a shape of light rather than a human expression — like something clicking into place.

HimEach kill strengthens the one who does it. At the expense of the other. You’ve known this since Chapter Seventeen and you didn’t tell me.

Me: “I told you what you needed to know when you needed to know it.”

HimYou told me nothing. You used it. You’ve been using it since you broke the agreement. You’ve been accumulating strength while I—”

Me: “While you had less control time and couldn’t do anything about it. Yes.”

The shared space held the weight of that. His light was dimmer than it had been when we began the treaty. Not critically — he was still present, still competing, still very much a force in the body we shared. But the gap was real and measurable and he could see it now as clearly as I could.

HimThen I’ll do the same. While I still have hours. I’ll use every one of them.

Me: “Go ahead.”

He went ahead.

During his control hours he killed. Not at the careful alternating rate of the treaty, not at the methodical pace we had developed together over months of practice, but at a pace driven by urgency, by the specific desperation of someone watching a gap close around them and trying to stop it by effort alone. He was trying to narrow the distance between our lights. He was trying to pull himself back to parity.

He was too late. I had too much of a head start, and my hours were longer than his now, and the longer my hours the more I could accumulate, and the more I accumulated the shorter his hours became. The system fed itself. Every kill I made during my increased control time reduced his control time, which reduced his ability to make kills, which reduced his ability to close the gap, which gave me more time, which gave me more kills.

I watched it happen with the specific, dissociated clarity of someone observing a process they have set in motion and cannot stop even if they want to. I was not sure I wanted to stop it. That was the honest answer. I was not sure.

His control hours shrank. From eight to six. From six to four. From four to two. He used every minute of them with a focused efficiency that I found, against my will, something close to respect for. He was fighting with everything available to him. He was doing exactly what I had done when I first arrived in this body and felt him pushing at the edges of my control. He was fighting to exist. That was all. Just fighting to exist in the only place left that would have him.

HimYou understand what you’re doing.

Me: “Yes.”

HimThis is the second time. You know that.

Me: “I know that.”

HimDoes it feel different?

I considered this honestly. The honest answer was the one I owed him, at minimum.

Me: “Yes. It feels worse.”

His light was very dim in the shared space now. Not gone. Still present, still him, still the specific quality of a person rather than a fading signal. But close.

HimFor what it’s worth. I don’t think you’re a bad person. I think you’re a person who keeps making the choice that keeps them alive. Same as me. Same as anyone.

Me: “That’s a generous reading.”

HimIt’s an accurate one. Generous would be saying you didn’t have a choice. You did. You just kept choosing the same direction.

His control hours reached zero on a Tuesday. There was no dramatic moment. His light in the shared space simply reached a level the body could not sustain alongside mine and then it was gone, and I was alone in the way I had been alone before any of this — except that I knew now what the aloneness was, what it had cost, what it had cost him specifically, and the knowing did not make the aloneness feel like freedom. It made it feel like a room with too much space in it.

(He is gone. I have killed him twice. Once with poison and a plan and the confidence of someone who thought death was a clean process. I sat in the room for a long time. The cracks continued outside. I could see them from the window — not many, not yet, the recent kills had bought time. But they were there. I came back. I ate dinner. I said goodnight to my parents. I went upstairs.

I did not write that in the journal. I thought it very clearly and then I closed the thought the way you close a door in a house you are still living in, knowing it will open again, knowing you will have to look at what is on the other side eventually.

Chapter Twenty

Final Choice

I woke up and the ceiling was familiar.

I got out of bed. Made it — flat and neat, corners tucked the way I had learned to like them. Went downstairs. The kitchen smelled like eggs and something sweet. Mother was humming something without a title. Father was reading. I sat down and ate breakfast slowly, tasting it properly, not because I was performing attention but because I had decided to actually be present for this. The weight of the fork. The quality of the morning light. The exact pitch of the hum.

Me: “Good morning.”

Mother looked up with her good-morning smile. Father nodded from behind his pages. Ordinary. Unaware. The breakfast table doing what breakfast tables do, completely indifferent to the fact that the person sitting at it had killed more people than he would ever count.

After breakfast I went to school. Walked the route. The broken tile on the corner. The house with the dog. No cracks today — not because they were gone but because I had spent the predawn hours closing the ones that had opened overnight. The work of it was familiar now, like any practiced task. I barely thought about what I was doing anymore. That was the part I tried not to think about too carefully.

I sat through lectures. I answered correctly. I walked past Old Friend A and Old Friend B in the hall and they waved and I waved back and there was a version of ease in it by now — not the ease of genuine belonging but the ease of long practice. The ease of knowing the choreography so well you can perform it without any awareness of performing.

After school. The park. The bench. The fountain.

This was where the days of normalcy ended, because I needed to be somewhere that was his before it was mine, to do what I had decided to do. I had been avoiding the third option for months. Not because I didn’t understand it. Because I understood it completely.

The necklace was in my pocket. It had been there all day, warm, patient, waiting.

I sat on the bench and looked at the fountain and let myself think about what I had thought I would never allow myself to think. Specifically. All the way to the end.

(The doubles are infinite. The realities are infinite. I am not. The kills slow the destruction but they do not stop it, and the number required to slow it grows faster than I can kill. This is not a problem I can solve by staying in it. The only person whose death closes a crack completely and permanently — not just seals it but removes it — is me. One version of me, stepping into the crack rather than through it. Into the fracture. Becoming the seal.)

(The necklace showed this. One person, one scene, different from all the others. They stepped in. And in the aftermath the other cracks — not all of them, not instantly, but visibly, measurably — dimmed.)

(I have been making the problem smaller by killing. I can make the problem end by going.)

The fountain ran. A bird. The late-afternoon light on the water doing what late-afternoon light does, indifferent and beautiful.

I thought about the breakfast table. I thought about my father’s uncomplicated pride and my mother’s hum without a title and the way Old Friend B’s worry had felt like something I didn’t deserve, aimed at a person who wasn’t there to receive it, and how I had tried to become that person anyway and how far I had managed to close the distance before the distance proved unclosable.

I thought about him. Not the twin as opponent, not the twin as the body’s competitor, but the boy in the mud in the photo album, age six, grinning with the specific whole-body confidence of a person who has not yet learned that the world can be unkind. The person my mother hummed for. The person my father’s pride had been aimed at for eighteen years.

I had not been able to be him. But I had been able to be here, in his place, long enough to understand what I had taken. That was the thing I had refused to look at and that I was now looking at directly, without blinking, because there was no reason left not to.

(I wanted a better life. I got one, and it was borrowed, and everything I touched in it was already someone else’s. And I did not deserve it, and I knew I did not deserve it, and I chose it anyway, and the choice broke something whose breakage I could not fix by staying.)

I took the necklace out of my pocket.

A crack appeared in the air above the fountain. Wide. Open longer than any I had seen. On its far side: my face, younger-looking, afraid. Not one of the mapped doubles. A new one. Someone who had just arrived and had not yet learned to be afraid quietly.

I stood. Walked toward the crack. Not toward him — the figure stepped back, uncertain — but toward the fracture itself. Toward the space between.

The necklace was warm in my closed hand. Warmer than it had ever been. As though it had always known this was where we were going and was ready for it.

———

What the sacrifice looked like from the outside, if anyone had been watching:

A person standing at the edge of a fountain in the late afternoon, holding something small in his closed hand. Then not standing there anymore. No transition visible. No light, no sound, no theatre. Just the bench, and the fountain, and the afternoon, and an absence shaped exactly like a person.

The crack above the fountain sealed immediately. Completely. Not the gradual narrowing of a mended fracture but an instant total closure, as though the fracture had never existed.

In the weeks that followed, the other cracks across the city dimmed. Slowed. Some closed entirely. The versions of people stopped appearing in hallways and parks and across streets. The air of the world went back to the ordinary quality of air that does not have something wrong with it.

Nobody knew why. Nobody had been able to see the problem in the first place. The solution was equally invisible. The world healed the way the world tends to: without acknowledgment, without ceremony, as though the injury had never been significant enough to name.

———

And on the other side of the choice:

No ceiling. No room. No morning light deciding which way to fall.

No family. No table. No hum without a title.

No friends whose grief could not find its target.

No other voice. No second current. No shared space, no equal light.

No versions of a face scattered across the surface of a cracking world.

No cracks.

No world to crack.

Just space. Unbounded. Unwitnessed. Without dimension or direction or the particular quality of being observed.

Just this.

Just here.

Just —

No one else. Not anymore. Only me.
